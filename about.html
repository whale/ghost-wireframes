About Ghost

What if a company had a mission
and actually stuck to it?

Ghost is a non-profit foundation building open source publishing tools. We've been at it since 2013. No investors, no shareholders, no exit strategy. Just a small team that thinks independent publishing matters.

How it started

In 2012, John was a WordPress core contributor — deputy head of design — who'd grown frustrated watching a publishing platform try to become everything at once. He wrote a blog post about what a focused publishing tool could look like if it was built without compromise. He called it Ghost.

The post went viral. It turned out a lot of people felt the same way.

He roped in Hannah Wolfe — his best friend and one of the most talented engineers he'd ever met — as co-founder and CTO. In April 2013, they launched a Kickstarter that raised $350,000 in 29 days. Ten times the goal.

By the end of that year, the first version of both the software and the managed hosting platform were live. Eleven months later, they were profitable.

The idea was simple: make a great open source product. Sell hosting for people who want the easiest experience. Use the revenue to keep making the product better. Repeat.

That's still the whole business model.

For-purpose, not for-profit

People hear "non-profit" and think we can't make money. That's not what it means. It means nobody owns the company. There are no shareholders. No equity. No one who can sell Ghost to the highest bidder.

Neither John nor Hannah own any shares, assets, domains, or trademarks related to Ghost. Everything belongs to the Foundation.

The practical effect: every dollar Ghost earns gets reinvested into Ghost. There's no one at the top siphoning profits or pushing for growth to justify a valuation. The company exists to serve the people who use it.

Every publishing platform eventually faces a moment where investors' returns and users' interests point in different directions. Ghost's structure means that moment never comes.

It's not a promise. It's a legal structure.

How we think about this

Small team, big ecosystem.

We don't want to be a giant company. We want to be a small team that supports a giant ecosystem of hosts, developers, agencies, and publishers. Running a big company doesn't seem very fun, tbh.

The software can't be sold. Neither can your trust.

Our non-profit constitution makes it legally impossible to sell Ghost. Not a handshake — a structure. Your publication will never wake up under new ownership with different priorities.

Open source means open.

MIT licensed. You can read every line of code, fork it, self-host it, or just know it's there. Nobody is required to use our hosting — the majority of Ghost websites in the world don't. The ones that do fund the project for everyone.

We don't tax your growth.

Ghost takes 0% of your subscription revenue. No percentage fees, no per-subscriber charges. Your audience gets bigger, your price stays the same. We make money from hosting, not from taxing your success.

Serious work, lightly held.

We care deeply about independent publishing. We don't take ourselves too seriously while doing it. Behind the scenes, we're a group of weird, fun-loving humans who enjoy building things and think this particular thing is worth building well.

35 people. That's the whole company.

Fully remote since day one. 5 continents, 16 nationalities, 15 languages. We've never had an office. We have a deliberate cap at about 50 people — because we'd rather know everyone's name than manage eight layers of hierarchy.

Where it all goes

Ghost(Pro) customers pay for hosting. That revenue goes to the Ghost Foundation. The Foundation pays the team, funds open source development, and invests in the ecosystem. That's the whole loop.

Illustration: revenue flow diagram
Ghost(Pro) customers → Foundation revenue → Team + open source + ecosystem → better product → more customers

We don't want to grow a giant company that we control. We want to grow a giant ecosystem that we support — hosts, developers, agencies, partners, and publishers building on top of shared infrastructure.

As Ghost grows, we intend to expand our board of trustees beyond the two founders. We want a governance structure that includes people who deeply understand open source, publishing, and journalism — with some seats elected by the Ghost community itself.

The goal is for Ghost to outlast its founders. That means building structures that don't depend on any one person — including us.
